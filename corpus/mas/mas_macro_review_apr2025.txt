6 Macroeconomic Review | April 2025
Key Messages
The Singapore economy faces headwinds from global trade tensions
The global economy grew steadily in 2024, underpinned by robust domestic demand in the
US and strong export performance in Asia. However, prospects for global GDP growth have
diminished amid the trade conflict. Economies that levy duties on imports will likely
experience an increase in costs, which will in turn weigh on business and consumer spending.
Meanwhile, exporting countries which have been hit by tariffs will experience a negative
external demand shock. As a whole, global GDP growth is expected to slow below trend.
Against this backdrop, Singapore’s growth outlook has turned more cautious. As a small and
open economy, Singapore will be heavily impacted by trade conflicts, with external-facing
industries such as manufacturing likely to see slower demand. The financial sector could see
reduced credit intermediation activity from a slowdown in investment spending, while a sharp
rise in risk-off sentiment could affect fees and commissions in the sector.
Growth in the Singapore economy is expected to slow to 0.0–2.0% in 2025 from 4.4% in 2024.
As a consequence, Singapore’s output gap will turn negative this year.
Inflation has fallen significantly and is expected to be low this year
Both MAS Core and CPI-All Items inflation fell significantly in Q1, reflecting to a large extent
the broad-based moderation in underlying price pressures. Soft domestic spending on F&B
services and retail goods as well as receding business cost pressures dampened the pace of
consumer price increases.
For the rest of 2025, domestic demand and cost pressures will be more moderate than
previously anticipated. Higher global tariffs are assessed to be disinflationary for Singapore
on net, as weaker economic activity abroad and in Singapore weighs on imported and
domestic cost increases.
Consequently, both MAS Core Inflation and CPI-All Items inflation are now projected to come
in lower than previously expected, averaging 0.5–1.5% in 2025. This is a decline from the 2.8%
and 2.4% recorded for core and headline inflation in 2024, respectively.
In April, MAS eased monetary policy by reducing the rate of appreciation of
the S$NEER policy band
MAS aims to secure low and stable inflation over the medium term by managing the path of
S$ nominal effective exchange rate (S$NEER) within a policy band.
In April 2025, MAS reduced the rate of appreciation of the S$NEER policy band slightly. There
was no change to the width of the policy band or the level at which it is centred. This
adjustment is consistent with a modest and gradual appreciation path of the S$NEER policy
band that will ensure medium-term price stability.

Key Messages 7
There is significant uncertainty over how trade policy actions will unfold in the period ahead.
Prolonged trade conflicts could pose further downside risks to growth, and thus inflation.
Conversely, trade tensions may de-escalate over time, which could boost sentiments and
export activity. MAS is closely monitoring these global and domestic economic
developments.

Monetary Policy Statement
INTRODUCTION
1. In January this year, MAS kept the Singapore dollar nominal effective exchange rate (S$NEER)
policy band on a modest and gradual appreciation path, but reduced its slope slightly. There was no
change to the width of the policy band or the level at which it was centred. Since then, the S$NEER
has fluctuated within the upper half of the policy band in response to shifts in the macroeconomic
outlook and shocks to global trade policies. Notwithstanding the volatility, the average level of the
S$NEER over the last three months has been broadly unchanged from that in the preceding three
months.
Chart 1
S$ Nominal Effective Exchange Rate (S$NEER)
GROWTH BACKDROP
2. Singapore’s key trading partners showed signs of weakening economic activity in the first
quarter of 2025. Consumer and business confidence in major economies have fallen alongside
escalating trade policy uncertainty. Concomitantly, retail sales and capex intentions have softened.
In the region, export performances across economies have turned more uneven, reflecting the
waning of temporary boosts to production and shipments from trade frontloading, even as underlying
AI-driven demand for electronics saw some resilience.
3. MTI’s Advance Estimates show that the Singapore economy expanded by 3.8% year-on-year
in Q1 2025. At the same time, the growth momentum was weaker than expected, contracting by 0.8%
on a quarter-on-quarter seasonally-adjusted basis, down from the 0.5% expansion a quarter ago. In
tandem with the nascent slowdown in some major economies, activity in the external-facing
manufacturing and modern services sectors declined. Meanwhile, the domestic-oriented sectors
saw generally tepid activity.

Monetary Policy Statement 9
4 . Prospects for global trade and GDP growth dimmed in early April. The US has imposed tariffs
on imports from most countries in the world, with some of these countries announcing retaliatory
tariffs. Economies that levy duties on imports will likely experience an increase in costs and this will
weigh on their aggregate demand. At the same time, exporting countries which have been hit by
tariffs will be confronted with weaker demand and pressure to lower prices for their output. In
addition, global financial conditions have tightened as asset markets have begun repricing risks in
the global economy. These factors will exert widespread and potentially reinforcing drags on
production, trade, and investments in Singapore’s major trading partners. Global growth is expected
to weaken this year, with trade possibly moderating to a greater extent.
5. For 2025 as a whole, Singapore’s GDP growth is expected to slow to 0.0–2.0% from 4.4% last
year. Given Singapore’s high trade dependency and deep integration with global supply chains,
slowing global and regional trade as well as heightened policy uncertainty will weigh on the external-
facing sectors, which could spill over into the domestic-oriented sectors. Consequently, the
aggregate level of output will come in below the economy’s potential this year.
6. The external environment remains uncertain. There are downside risks to Singapore’s
economic outlook stemming from episodes of financial market volatility and a sharper-than-
expected fall in final demand abroad. A more abrupt or persistent weakening in global trade will have
significant ramifications on Singapore’s trade-related sectors, and in turn, the broader economy.
INFLATION OUTLOOK
7. MAS Core Inflation1 eased significantly to 0.7% y-o-y in Jan–Feb 2025, from 1.9% in Q4 2024.
Inflation fell by more than expected across a wide range of goods and services. Soft consumer
spending on F&B services and retail goods domestically, as well as moderating cost pressures, have
dampened consumer price increases. Enhanced government subsidies also contributed to lower
services inflation. The re-basing of the CPI in January this year accounted for only a small part of the
step down in inflation.
8. MAS Core Inflation is now forecast to average 0.5–1.5% in 2025, down from 1.0–2.0% in the
January 2025 MPS. The downgrade reflects the lower-than-expected inflation outturns in Jan–Feb
as well as an anticipated moderation in the pace of price increases amid the weakening economic
outlook. On the external front, imported inflation should be modest, as slowing global demand and
lower energy commodity prices dampen imported costs. Domestic unit labour cost increases are
also expected to moderate due to easing nominal wage growth and improving labour productivity.
Together with softer consumer spending, as well as enhanced government subsidies, these factors
should temper inflation in the quarters ahead.
9. CPI-All Items inflation in 2025 is similarly expected to average 0.5–1.5%, down from 1.5–2.5%
previously. This reflects in part the low outturns in Jan–Feb as well as the downgrade to the MAS
Core Inflation forecast range.
MONETARY POLICY
10. Amid the weakening external outlook, Singapore’s output gap will turn negative. Consequently,
imported and domestic cost pressures will remain low and MAS Core Inflation is forecast to stay
well below 2%. The risks to inflation are tilted towards the downside.
1 MAS Core Inflation excludes the costs of accommodation and private transport from CPI-All Items inflation.

11. MAS will continue with the policy of a modest and gradual appreciation of the S$NEER policy
band. However, the rate of appreciation will be reduced slightly. There will be no change to the width

12. MAS will closely monitor global and domestic economic developments, and remain vigilant to
risks to inflation and growth.

Financial Conditions 11
1 Financial Conditions
1.1 Global Developments
Sentiments in global financial markets have weakened since early April
Following the US imposition of tariffs worldwide in early April, global financial market volatility
and risk aversion have risen sharply. Since 2 April, the MSCI All Country Index has fallen by
5%1, bringing losses since the beginning of the year to 6%. The prices of growth-sensitive
commodities like crude oil and copper have also fallen (Chart 1.1), indicating that global
growth prospects have dimmed. As a measure of the heightened degree of risk aversion in
markets, the VIX rose sharply to a level that had previously only been breached during the
COVID-19 pandemic and the Global Financial Crisis (Chart 1.2).

In contrast to previous global risk-off episodes, US Treasury yields rose sharply from 2 April.
At the same time, the US dollar has depreciated against most currencies, in particular the
Euro, Japanese yen and Swiss franc. On the whole, the Nominal Broad US Dollar Index has
fallen by around 9% since the beginning of the year (Chart 1.3). In April alone, it depreciated
by 4% amid the increasingly pessimistic assessment of global trade policies and their likely
impact on the US economy.

Global monetary policy has remained on a broad easing path. While the US Federal Reserve
paused its series of rate cuts in December 2024, US 2-year yields have moderated since

rate to 2.25%, while the Bank of Japan paused its rate hike cycle. In the region, the 7-day

1.2 Domestic Developments
The S$NEER has fluctuated within the upper half of the policy band, but its
average level has remained broadly unchanged since the start of 2025
Amid the financial market volatility, the S$NEER has fluctuated in the upper half of the policy
band. It appreciated in January amid broad-based US$ weakness, but depreciated in early
April as global risk aversion rose, and on market expectations that MAS would ease monetary
policy (Chart 1.5). Over the last three months, the S$ has strengthened against the US dollar,
the Indonesian rupiah, Chinese renminbi and Australian dollar, while depreciating against the
Japanese yen, Swiss franc, and the Euro (Chart 1.6). On average, the level of the S$NEER over
the last three months has remained broadly unchanged from that in the preceding three
months.

2 The International Economy
2.1 Recent Developments
Global growth momentum expanded at a firm pace in 2024
The global economy expanded at a steady pace in Q4 last year, bringing overall growth for
2024 to a creditable 3.2%1 (Chart 2.1). The two key growth drivers for the year—robust
domestic demand in the US and strong export performance in Asia—had underpinned the Q4
outturn (Chart 2.2). The US economy saw strong consumer spending alongside continued
investment in ICT equipment and software. Meanwhile, the strength of US final demand
combined with frontloading by US importers in anticipation of tariffs, had lent support to the
production and export activities of China and ASEAN.

2.2 Outlook
The sharp escalation in trade frictions has negatively impacted prospects for
global trade and growth
The escalation of trade frictions, involving the increase of import tariffs by the US and
retaliatory measures by some of its trade partners, will have serious ramifications for the
global economy. The US is deeply plugged into global trade, accounting for almost a quarter
of flows in the world’s trade corridors (Chart 2.5).
Economies that levy duties on imports will likely experience an increase in costs, which will
in turn weigh on business and consumer spending. Meanwhile, exporting countries which
have been hit by the US tariffs will experience a negative externally induced shock on export
production. Beyond the impact from trade channels, heightened policy uncertainty and the
broader tightening in financial conditions will cause businesses and households to defer
investment and discretionary spending, exerting further downward pressure on growth.
The uncertainty surrounding the global growth outlook remains elevated. Global growth could
slip further if the US broadens and intensifies the tariffs to include other specific products
such as semiconductors and pharmaceuticals. Retaliatory actions by its trading partners will
exacerbate the impact on growth. Market sentiments also remain fragile, and further shocks
could trigger disorderly price adjustments, with the ensuing tightening in financial conditions
compounding the downturn.
Global GDP growth is expected to slow significantly to between 2.0–2.5% in 2025, from 3.2%
last year. There is significant uncertainty around US trade policy and its impact.
Price pressures should remain contained notwithstanding tariff-induced price
increases in the US
Global headline inflation is projected to average 2.3% in 2025, slightly higher than the 2.1%
seen last year. In the US, the tariffs will impart a fresh and significant shock to prices, even as
its growth moderates and wage growth eases amid elevated concerns about the negative
demand impact.
However, the impact on price pressures should remain contained as the rest of the world
faces downward price pressures amid the tariff-induced negative demand shock. Commodity
prices are expected to ease due to softer global demand and positive supply-side factors,
such as the planned oil supply increases by OPEC countries for crude oil and favourable
weather conditions for food production.
Within the region, China’s disinflationary pressures could persist. This reflects subdued
consumer confidence and significant economic slack. Domestic price pressures in ASEAN
will also remain modest in view of below-trend growth.

3.1 Recent Developments
Growth in the Singapore economy eased in Q1 2025
Economic activity in Singapore showed signs of softening at the start of this year. Based on
the Advance Estimates, Singapore’s GDP contracted by 0.8% q-o-q SA in Q1 2025, a pullback
from the 0.5% expansion in Q4 2024 (Chart 3.1). On a y-o-y basis, GDP growth moderated to
3.8% from 5.0% in the previous quarter. The weaker performance was driven by the trade-
related and modern services sectors, while activity in the domestic-facing industries such as
construction and retail were generally tepid1 (Chart 3.2). While there are nascent indications
of economic softening at the sectoral level, these weaknesses have yet to become deeply
entrenched or pervasive.

3.2 Outlook
The external global economic landscape has become more uncertain
Singapore’s growth outlook has turned more cautious amid an increasingly challenging global
trade environment. On 2 April, the US imposed reciprocal tariffs of at least 10% on nearly all
countries, with exemptions on certain products. Singapore was subjected to the 10% baseline
tariff rate, while various other countries were hit by higher tariffs. Shortly after on 9 April, a 90-
day pause was announced on the higher-band tariffs except those imposed on China, but the
baseline 10% tariff and the 25% tariff on all US imports of steel, aluminium, and automobiles
& parts implemented earlier remain in place.
The impact of the tariffs will propagate through multiplier effects and generate a broader
negative income and demand shock to the Singapore economy. GDP growth will also be
impacted through the confidence channel by the continuing uncertainty surrounding the
eventual effective tariff, which will induce a broader retraction in global consumption and
investment spending. (See Box A on an empirical analysis of the impact of policy uncertainty
on the Singapore economy). The transmissions to production, income, consumption and
ultimately overall GDP growth for Singapore will occur through three main stages:
• The increase in the global tariff rate is akin to a production tax on Singapore’s
producers and exporters who are price takers in world markets. With Singapore’s
terms of trade (excluding oil) already weakening since mid-2024, additional tariffs
would only exacerbate the strain by squeezing export margins.
• Singapore firms then reduce their production levels as the short-term profit
maximising level of output falls. Consequently, domestic producers are hit by falling
corporate incomes/profits, which could lead to less demand for factors of
production.

Box A: The Impact of Policy Uncertainty on the Singapore Economy
The Global Economic Policy Uncertainty (GEPU) index (Baker, Bloom, and Davis, 2016)
reached an all-time high in early 2025 (Chart A1), predominantly reflecting a surge in
uncertainty over US trade policy. Apart from the trade-dampening effects of the implemented
tariffs itself, uncertainty around the scale, scope and permanence of trade barriers could also
impact business and consumer spending. Firms are likely to hold back on their investments
due to the increased level of uncertainty (Dixit and Pindyck, 1994), while borrowing costs
could also rise with greater risk premia. Meanwhile, households may reduce spending to build
up precautionary buffers against potential income shocks. This Box explores the quantitative
effects of global policy uncertainty on the Singapore economy.

Impulse Response Functions
The impulse response functions (IRFs) from the SVAR model indicate that global policy
uncertainty has statistically significant adverse impacts on the Singapore economy. In
response to a positive one standard deviation (SD) shock to the GEPU index (or a 20.4%
increase in uncertainty), private non-residential investment registers a cumulative 3.6%
decline after four quarters while private consumption falls 1.5% within the same period (Chart
A2). Overall, real GDP is 1.3% below baseline a year after the shock—similar to the 1.0%
estimated by Luk et al. (2017) for Hong Kong but larger than the 0.5% estimated by the
European Commission (2024) for the Eurozone.2

impact on Singapore’s GDP is greatest four quarters after the shock. Notably, leveraging on

impact of uncertainty. GDP falls by a meaningful extent after a significant increase in
uncertainty but shows no statistically significant response to declines (Chart A3).

Sum-Up
The analysis in this Box suggests that the sharp rise in global policy uncertainty since
late last year will weigh on Singapore’s growth. Private non-residential investment is likely to
pull back by more than consumption. At the same time, the tightening of global financial
conditions could reinforce the drag on real economic activity in Singapore.

Box B: Macroeconomic Assessment of Budget 2025
This Box item provides an overview and assessment of the key economic initiatives in
Budget 2025.
Macroeconomic Backdrop
Budget 2025 was delivered in February in the context of relative macroeconomic balance,
compared to the previous years of high inflation and volatile growth. At the end of 2024, GDP
was estimated to be slightly above the economy’s potential, while labour market conditions
had largely normalised following a period of tightness. In addition, inflation had fallen
significantly with MAS Core Inflation coming in below 2% y-o-y after an extended period of
elevated inflation.
Nonetheless, elevated costs and prices had remained a concern. In particular, while real
wages in Singapore grew faster over 2020–24 than in many regional economies as well as
some advanced economies, the pace of real wage gains has slowed slightly compared to the
pre-pandemic period (2010–19). Further, heightened geopolitical and trade policy uncertainty
posed additional downside risks to Singapore’s small and trade-dependent economy, and
consequently jobs and livelihoods. Indeed, Singapore’s GDP growth is expected to slow in
2025 amid tariff-related weakening in external demand and heightened uncertainty. The
balance of risks was therefore tilted towards growth weakening by more than expected, and
in turn inflation falling more discernibly.
Near-term Support Measures to Alleviate Cost Pressures
Against this backdrop, Budget 2025 sought to address near-term cyclical concerns as
well as longer-term priorities.
The Budget provided further support for coping with cost-of-living pressures, especially
to lower- and middle-income households. For instance, there were additional Community
Development Council (CDC) Vouchers for all Singaporean households, U-Save rebates to help
with utilities expenses for eligible HDB households, as well as enhanced support for lower-
income households under the ComCare Assistance schemes.
This year’s Budget also included an SG60 package to recognise the contributions of all
Singaporeans, and to share the benefits of the nation’s progress. The package included SG60
Vouchers for all Singaporeans aged 21 and above this year, as well as a Personal Income Tax
Rebate of 60% for the Year of Assessment 2025, capped at $200, to mostly benefit middle-
income workers.
In a similar vein to previous Budgets, Budget 2025 also continued to provide support for
firms grappling with higher costs. The Progressive Wage Credit Scheme was enhanced, by
increasing the Government’s co-funding levels for wage increases. A 50% Corporate Income
Tax Rebate was also introduced for the Year of Assessment 2025, capped at $40,000, with
every active company that employed at least one local employee last year receiving a
minimum benefit of $2,000.

The Singapore Economy 29
Structural Measures to Sustain Growth and Inclusivity
Aside from addressing near-term concerns, the Budget continued to build on ongoing
efforts to strengthen the supply-side of the economy by enhancing labour quality and building
up the capital stock.
To boost the potential of the resident workforce, the Budget enhanced the Workfare
Skills Support (WSS) scheme, to provide lower-wage workers aged 30 years and above with
monthly training allowances when they enrol in selected long-form part-time or full-time
courses. This policy will help to alleviate the sharp income trade-off that lower-wage workers
face when they forgo work to upskill themselves.
To bolster the capital stock, a significant sum ($19.6 billion, 2.6% of GDP) was set aside
to top up funds that will finance spending into the medium term on critical areas such as
preparing the nation for climate change, exploring clean energies, investing in productivity
and R&D initiatives, as well as the construction of Changi Airport Terminal 5. The use of fund
top-ups secures the Government’s ability to continue with investments in the Singapore
economy, to support long-term needs in a sustainable way, regardless of shifting cyclical
conditions.
Budget 2025 also enhanced efforts to reduce inequality, support families and improve
social mobility—important priorities for Singaporeans, as revealed by the Forward Singapore
exercise. The Budget enhanced the Fresh Start Housing Scheme, which helps families with
children living in public rental flats to own their own homes by buying a shorter-lease new flat
with a housing grant. There were also measures to reduce preschool expenses, provide more
support to large families with three or more children, and improve the affordability of long-
term care expenses. Labour policies that incentivise employers to hire more vulnerable
groups who might experience greater difficulties finding employment, such as seniors, ex-
offenders, and persons with disabilities, were also extended.
Quantitative Assessment of Budget 2025
On a calendar year basis, the fiscal impulse (FI), which gauges the net fiscal support to
GDP from the change in the cyclically adjusted budget balance, is estimated to be a modest
+0.4% of GDP.1 This is a mildly supportive impulse for the economy in 2025.
To quantify the impact of Budget 2025 in a general equilibrium context and account for
its dynamic effects, key measures were simulated using the Monetary Model of Singapore
(MMS), MAS’ flagship macroeconomic model.
Budget 2025 is estimated to lift real disposable incomes by about 1% in 2025 through
the SG60 package and cost relief measures. This will boost the level of real GDP by about 0.4%
(Chart B1), mainly through stronger consumption growth compared to a counterfactual
scenario without these discretionary fiscal injections.

4.2 Inflation Developments
Inflation slowed further and came in below expectations in Q1
MAS Core Inflation fell significantly to 0.6% y-o-y in Q1 from 1.9% in Q4 2024, while CPI-All
Items inflation moderated to 1.0% from 1.4% (Chart 4.7). In both instances, the pace of price
increases was weaker than expected. The decline in core inflation in Q1 compared to the
previous quarter was driven to a large extent by a broad-based weakening in underlying price
pressures.
The low inflation prints reflect a strengthening of the disinflation trend that began in mid-
2024. By Q4 last year, inflation momentum had fallen to low levels. It has since weakened
further, turning negative in Q1 (Chart 4.8). Alternative measures of underlying inflation that
abstract from more volatile price components also indicated continued disinflation.

Meanwhile, business cost pressures have also remained relatively modest. In recent months,

On balance, higher global tariffs are expected to exert broad-based disinflationary impulses.
While the US imposition of higher import duties on nearly all its trading partners will lift
imported inflation in the US, its passthrough to consumer prices locally should be limited by
Singapore’s relatively modest imports of consumer goods from the US. At the same time,
some global exports originally destined for the US may be redirected to countries that have
not raised import duties, such as Singapore, increasing the supply of goods and lowering
import prices for the domestic economy. Further, weaker global aggregate demand will weigh
on the prices of commodities and manufactured goods, exerting demand-driven
disinflationary pressure on Singapore’s import costs.
With the downgrade in Singapore’s GDP growth forecast, domestic demand and cost
pressures are also expected to moderate. The easing in aggregate labour demand will exert
a drag on nominal wage growth and thus discretionary spending, which, together with the
projected negative output gap, will further dampen inflationary pressures. Growth in services
ULC (adjusted by trend productivity) is also forecast to be slower than last year, and below its
historical average.
All in, the anticipated slippage in global and domestic demand will temper inflation across
several broad CPI components for the year as a whole. In particular, falling global crude oil
prices will cause electricity and gas prices to decline, while the anticipated below-trend
services ULC growth will dampen discretionary and essential services inflation (Chart 4.13).
Cost passthrough to prices should also be mitigated by soft consumer demand.
The risks to the inflation outlook are tilted to the downside. The near-term disinflationary
effects of higher global tariffs for Singapore could intensify if the associated negative
demand effects turn out to be larger or more long-lasting than expected.
